Introduction

Menopause is a natural biological phase in a woman’s life when monthly periods permanently stop. It usually happens between the age of 45–55 years. Marking the end of a woman's reproductive years due to declining ovarian hormone production, primarily estrogen, typically around age 51 but varying from 45-55, involving stages like Perimenopause (transition with irregular periods, hot flashes, mood swings), Menopause (the specific moment of final period), and Postmenopause (years after, with potential long-term risks like osteoporosis and heart disease).

Menopause is not a disease, it is a transition in life.

All women experience changes to their bodies as they age. You probably remember the first big biological change, marked by the arrival of your first period. This is the only easy-to-identify external sign that you’ve formally entered into reproductive aging, technically termed “menarche”. We are all growing older every day, but many women don’t realize that medically, there is a reproductive clock that ticks alongside, but not always in sync with, their biological clock (despite the common use of ‘the biological clock’ indicating how much time a woman has left to bear children). It is this reproductive aging process that determines when you enter into perimenopause, menopause and finally, settle into post menopausal life.

Perimenopause

Perimenopause is the time before menopause when your body is getting ready to stop having periods. Perimenopause is sometimes called the menopausal transition. Women start perimenopause at different ages. You may notice signs, such as your periods becoming irregular, sometime in your 40s. But some women notice changes as early as their 30s or as late as their 50s. During perimenopause, estrogen the main female hormone in your body rises and falls. Your periods may get longer or shorter. You also may have symptoms such as hot flashes, trouble sleeping and vaginal dryness. Once you've gone 12 months in a row without a menstrual period, you've reached menopause and perimenopause is over.

During perimenopause, you may notice changes in your body. You might have, Irregular periods, Sleep problems, Mood changes, Vaginal and bladder problems, Decreasing fertility, Loss of bone, Changing cholesterol levels.

Menopause

The transition into menopause usually starts with changes in women’s menstrual cycle patterns, often accompanied by hot flashes or other symptoms. Most women begin the menopausal transition between ages 45 and 55, and the process may last for several years. Women reach menopause at different ages, and the average age of menopause is 51. You will only know that you’ve reached menopause when you have not had a period or spotting for a full year. During the menopausal transition, the body’s production of estrogen and progesterone, declines greatly. Changes in these hormones may explain the symptoms that some women experience during this time. Symptoms related to menopause can last for between two and eight years, though the timing and symptoms are highly variable among women. These factors are likely influenced by a woman’s genetics as well as external factors, such as race and ethnicity, culture, lifestyle, and environment. During transition the distribution of fat in the body changes, and women may gain weight more easily. They may also experience changes in their bone density, heart health, body shape and composition, or physical function. Menopause symptoms result from falling hormone levels (estrogen and progesterone), typically causing irregular periods, hot flashes, night sweats, sleep disruptions, and mood changes. Other common symptoms include vaginal dryness, lower libido, weight gain, thinning hair, and memory "brain fog". These symptoms Not all women will experience menopausal symptoms when they go through the menopause, up to 80-90% will have some symptoms, with 25% describing them as severe and debilitating. The most common symptoms are hot flushes and night sweats (vasomotor symptoms) , experienced by 70-80% of women.

Post Menopause

Postmenopause is a term to describe the time after you’ve gone through menopause. When you’re in postmenopause (or postmenopausal), your menstrual period has been gone for longer than 12 consecutive months. At this stage in life, your reproductive years are behind you and you’re no longer ovulating (or releasing eggs). Uncomfortable symptoms of menopause like hot flashes and night sweats may become milder or go away completely in postmenopause. People in postmenopause are at an increased risk for osteoporosis because their bodies don’t make a lot of estrogen, which is essential for maintaining strong and healthy bones.
You lose bone density more rapidly after menopause due to decreased levels of estrogen. You can lose 25% of your bone density or more after menopause (approximately 1% to 2% per year).
People in postmenopause may experience moodiness, anxiety and depression. Things like stress, sexual tension or other life challenges can cause mental health disruptions. Hormone levels can also affect your mood. It might help to talk with a therapist or counselor about what you’re feeling to see if they can offer ways to cope with this new phase of your life. After transitioning through menopause many doctors recommends various types of therapy to ease symptoms and prevent or manage ongoing conditions that may happen with aging which includes Menopause Hormone Therapy, Coping Methods for Menopause, Exercise & Fitness, Non-Hormone Medications, Menopause Supplements, Naturopathic Therapy, Talk Therapy, Music Therapy, etc.

Menopause Help

Regular exercise, a balanced diet rich in calcium and protein, good sleep habits, and stress management can greatly help during menopause. Medical options like hormone replacement therapy (HRT), music therapy or medicines may reduce hot flashes and mood swings, while supplements such as calcium and vitamin D support bone health. Staying hydrated, limiting caffeine and spicy foods, and seeking emotional support from family, friends, or counseling can also make this phase more comfortable and manageable

Menopause Therapy

There are various therapies to help ease the symptoms of menopause, such as Hormone Replacement Therapy (HRT), Systemic Estrogen Therapy , Vaginal (Local) Estrogen Therapy, Non-Hormonal Medications, Lifestyle Therapy, Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT), Bone Protection Therapy, Music therapy, Talk therapy, Nutritional therapy and much more but consult a good doctor with a keen knowledge about these therapies and about menopause.

Menopause Symptoms

Hot Flashes
Sudden heat in face, neck and chest. May cause sweating and redness.
Night Sweats
Heavy sweating during sleep that disturbs rest.
Irregular periods
Periods become lighter, heavier or unpredictable.
Mood Swings
Irritability, anxiety or sudden emotional shifts.
Sleep Problems
Trouble falling asleep or waking up often.
Vaginal Dryness
Dryness and discomfort due to lower estrogen.
Weight Gain
Slower metabolism may increase abdominal fat.
Brain Fog
Memory lapses and difficulty concentrating.

Precautions

Maintain a balanced diet Good nutrition is a essential for managing menopause symptoms. Foods to include in your diet are:
Fruits and vegetables : These are full of the vitamins, minerals, and antioxidants your body needs.
Fibre-rich foods and calcium & vitamin D : High-fibre foods like leafy greens, kidney beans, and whole grains, dairy products, omega-3 fatty acids are good sources of nutrition.
Stay active : As your body changes, regular movement can help you stay healthy and strengthen your bones, boost your mood, and counter symptoms such as weight gain.
Cardio : Cardio include endurance activities that encourage you to use your large muscles.
Strength training : Lifting dumbbells or using weight machines can help strengthen your muscles and bones, while also reducing body fat.
Yoga : Yoga is a source of targeted symptom relief, helping relax the body. Paired with meditation or breathing exercises, these can also prompt relaxation and mindfulness.
Keeping your mental health in check also helps a lot with menopause.
Avoid Fatty meats & processed foods, alcohol, Caffeine as these can worsen the symptoms caused by menopause

Point of This Campaign

Menopause is one of the most overlooked and misunderstood phases of the human body. It is an extremely important transition in the life of every woman which mostly occurs around mid 40s. At the same time it is also important for younger individuals to understand more about menopause and its effects on the body. This campaign was created to shed some light and educate as many people as possible about menopause and it's various stages. By doing so individuals who are unaware will gain a better understanding of this transition and become more socially conscious about it. As shown in the website, a nealry half of the women going through menopause transition have very little knowledge about pre menopause or menopause in general. So campaigns like this are needed to spread awareness about it. The goal is not only to support women who are currently experiencing or will experience menopause in the future but also to educate society as a whole so that menopause is better understood, accepted, and openly discussed.
